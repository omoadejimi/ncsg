## Accessibility is the Nigerian default

Globally, accessibility is often framed around disability alone. In Nigeria the constraints stack: disability, plus low-cost devices, plus metered data, plus interfaces in a second language. Writing accessibly serves all four at once.

Aim for [WCAG 2.2 AA](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/) as the floor, not the ceiling.

## Writing for screen readers

- **Link text describes the destination.** "View your statement", never "click here".
- **Buttons say what they do.** "Send ₦5,000", not "Submit" or "Continue" where a specific verb fits.
- **Headings form a real outline.** One h1 per page; don't skip levels. Screen-reader users navigate by headings the way sighted users scan.
- **Alt text says what matters.** Describe the information the image carries, not its appearance. Decorative images get empty alt text.
- **Avoid ALL CAPS and stylised Unicode.** Both can be read out letter by letter or skipped entirely.

## Writing for low bandwidth and low-end devices

- Front-load every page and message; assume the bottom may never load.
- Keep transactional SMS and push copy short — they may be the only channel that arrives.
- Don't put essential information only in images.

## Writing numbers, codes and references

- Break long codes into groups: **0803 123 4567**, not 08031234567.
- Let people copy reference numbers with one tap, and say so: "Tap to copy".

## Plain language is an accessibility feature

Cognitive accessibility — for people with dyslexia, low literacy or simply a stressful moment — depends on the same techniques as [plain language](/docs/foundations/plain-language). They are one practice.

<Note>
When testing, include at least one session over a throttled 3G connection on a sub-₦80,000 Android device. If the content survives that, it survives anything.
</Note>
